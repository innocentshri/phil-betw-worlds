The Foundations

Long-form primers that begin with an invitation: what is being asked, why it matters, and how to think about it. Each primer includes an interpretive reading and a set of practice prompts for active study.

On Questions

Philosophy begins with careful questioning — not to produce final answers, but to refine the map. A good question separates conceptual confusions and points to the evidence that would change minds.

Tip
"Good philosophical questions are diagnostic: they reveal hidden assumptions."
Practice: take a familiar claim and list what it presupposes.

Methods: Reason, Experience, and Insight

This primer compares analytic argumentation, historical-contextual critique, empirical support, and meditative insight. Readers are asked to examine which methods a claim relies on, and whether those methods are sufficient.

Practice prompts: reconstruct an argument in premise→conclusion form; test its counterexamples.

The Great Minds

Deep profiles: each entry is exploratory — biography, central problems, core arguments, and a sustained critical reading. The collection balances Western (≈70%) and Indian (≈30%) thinkers, organized to encourage cross-cultural conversation.

Plato c.427–347 BCE · Forms, Justice

Plato’s dialogues probe what a just life and a just polis might look like. The analytic move is to separate appearance from structure: are the social roles he sketches descriptive or prescriptive?

Reflective prompt: how would Plato's republic treat dissent? What modern institutions echo Platonic ideas?

Aristotle 384–322 BCE · Causes, Virtue Ethics

Aristotle builds a naturalistic account of ethics and science. His teleology is often misunderstood: it's not mystical but a way to describe organized complexity.

Reflective prompt: test Aristotelian teleology against modern developmental biology.

Immanuel Kant 1724–1804 · Epistemology & Ethics

Kant’s critical project asks: what structures must be in place for experience to be possible? Ethically, his universalizability test offers a distinctive way to evaluate duties.

Reflective prompt: find a moral rule you hold — can it be universalized without contradiction?

Śaṅkara Advaita Vedānta · Non-dualism

Śaṅkara’s non-dualism posits Brahman as ultimate reality and interprets scriptural testimony through rigorous dialectic. The challenge is to reconcile soteriological claims with epistemic standards acceptable to a rational reader.

Reflective prompt: what would count as evidence for a non-dual metaphysical claim?

Nāgārjuna Madhyamaka · Emptiness

Nāgārjuna’s method dissolves reified categories by demonstrating their dependence. The philosophical consequence is to shift focus from ontological claims to pragmatic and linguistic clarity.

Reflective prompt: apply the tetralemma to a modern metaphysical claim.

Charvaka Materialist · Empiricism

Charvaka insists on perception as the only reliable cognition and rejects unverifiable metaphysics. Its clarity is a corrective: demand evidence and be explicit about burden of proof.

Reflective prompt: identify contemporary metaphysical claims that require extraordinary evidence.

Book Summaries

Extended summaries: thesis, argumentative arc, and a critical reading that points to strengths, weaknesses, and study suggestions.

Plato — Republic (Extended)

Republic stages a wide-ranging conversation about justice, education, and the good. Read as a sustained experiment: what political institutions produce virtue, and at what cost?

Study tip: trace the argument across the dialogue — notice where Socratic elenchus halts or redirects an interlocutor.

Selected Upanishads (Extended)

Upanishadic texts combine metaphysical declarations with lived contemplative practices. Read them alongside pragmatic analyses: which claims are phenomenological, and which are doctrinal?

Study tip: identify experiential claims and test them via interpretive comparison.
